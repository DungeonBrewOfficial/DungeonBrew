    Dheghom

    Dreams of the Grimdark

    The world of Dheghom [THEG-om] has a history filled with war, plague, and misery that has darkened the outlook of many of its peoples. Violence is all they know, with betrayals and long wars that have left festering wounds which even time cannot heal.

    Rumors abound of the heinous acts of the despotic rulers of the most influential lands, while the most altruistic have become belabored with the long fatigue of the world and only seek to hide away from the reality of it. The psychological anguish of living on Dheghom has driven the best to isolation, leaving the rest of the world to devour itself.

    Magic is an ancient and powerful force that comes with a steep price. In modernized lands, magic is thought to either be a myth or lost to time and any display of it is met with fear and, often, violence from the assumption of its evil. In these places, steampower technology has taken the place of magic. When true magic is discovered, it has unforeseeable and far-reaching consequences. One who binds themselves to such power can rarely put it down willingly.

    Both divine and arcane magic are seen as either evil or children's tales to most modern and civilized peoples. Even the churches of the modern world, even the most well-meaning, have no expectation that the "miracles" of the past ages will come again and any claiming or displaying such gifts are likely evil themselves, seeking to steal the power and authority of the true divine.

    Heroes are unheard of. There are mercenaries, there are scavengers, and there are thieves. Any adventurer is merely one of those three in disguise. There are those who have been beaten into submission and those who will betray their patron at their first opportunity. The truly good, right, and just of Dheghom must always have some ulterior motive.

    Still, those adventurers come in all shapes and sizes, from the Oni of Kimara to the ogres of the Tribes of Gane, from the rarely seen Tengu of Sharasa to the mighty Cyclops of Ostiros, if there is profit to be had, they are seen adventuring.

    Dheghom acknowledges the role of a true adventurer, who only seeks their own fortunes and power. It is said that in every tale there are winners and losers, but in Dheghom, even the victories are Pyrrhic ones. Invariably, the consequences of any actions are far-reaching and dangerous in ways that cannot be fathomed, even by those with the best of intentions.

    Using this Book

    This book introduces Dheghom, steampunk grimdark setting for your campaign. It guides players and Game Masters through the process of creating characters, deific beings, and adventures set in this grimdark, war-torn world.

    Chapter 1 details the history of Dheghom, from its origins through to the current state of the world. From this history, the development of current technology, the degradation of magic, and the corruption of the gods is explored.

    Chapter 2 explores the lands and cultures of Dheghom, providing a comprehensive look at their histories, conflicts, and belief systems, offering inspiration for character backstories and adventure settings alike.

    Chapter 3 introduces Reputation as a mechanic for Dheghom factions. In addition, the most powerful factions in Dheghom, their motivations, hierarchies, and story hooks are provided.

    Chapter 4 provides new species, lineages, subclasses and feats for players along with a new look at alignment for a grimdark setting.

    Chapter 5 offers unique items, equipment, concoctions, and powerful steam tools to use in your next adventure or campaign along with rules for managing these powerful technologies.

    Chapter 6 introduces new monsters and NPCs, inspired by the grimdark setting.

    Welcome to Dheghom, a world where survival is never guaranteed, and every choice has lasting consequences.

    What to Know

    Dheghom is unlike many other worlds, and there are some important pieces of lore you should be aware of when considering how to approach character creation, adventures, and campaigns. While the book itself will delve deeply into many of these concepts, this brief overview should help you to get a sense of what you are getting yourself into.

    1. Closed System

    Dheghom was specifically created by the gods to be closed from the rest of the typical fantasy multiverse in order to protect themselves and their creations. As part of their creation, they placed themselves within the confines of the world and exist as physical entities with divine souls. They believed they could act as fonts of energy to sustain the world they created, but their power is waning as more of them become corrupted.

    2. War is Eternal

    War is a reflection of the corruption within the system the gods created. Where there is not an active war, there is a cold war. This reality is compounded by powerful factions that hold the power of nations themselves, often driving conflict for their own benefit.

    3. Magic is Mistrusted

    Magic is fueled by the gods themselves, by the energy that created Dheghom. However, like all other pieces of this world, the corruption of the gods has twisted the magic as well. While some cultures have fond memories of the old magics, the industrialized nations exist as a way to compensate for that missing power. Common people are terrified of all forms of magic and when an individual is suspected of practicing dark arts, they react violently, oftentimes with the law on their side.

    4. Technology is the Future

    The introduction of black powder has changed the warscape. The sound of war has changed. In the same way that the technology of the crossbow allowed the unskilled to kill with little training, black powder has redoubled the deadliness of war. The fear and paranoia that infests Dheghom means that a loaded pistol is not far from reach for those who can afford it, and the violence that once only filled the fields of battle has begun to spill into the streets of the grand cities.

    5. A Dying World

    The conflagration of plagues, natural disasters, war, magical chaos, and general devastation across the face of Dheghom has convinced many of its inhabitants that the end times are coming. Whether or not this is true, such beliefs have resulted in a new wave of panic that has caused the borders of great nations to wither under the tumult of prophetic vagrants, mob rule, and violent fanatics.

    6. The Weight of History

    The memory of better times has become clouded, even among the longer-lived species. However, those who still speak with the gods have a sense of what has been lost, even as they try to protect them. Meanwhile, gods that have been corrupted in ages past now ravage the lands, fueled by hatred, fury, and despair.

    Grimdark in Your Game

    Dheghom is a world without heroes. Its lands are scarred by war, its rulers are corrupt, and survival is often the only reward. To run a grimdark campaign in Dheghom is to immerse players in a setting where every decision carries weight and every triumph is paid for in blood.

    As the GM, you are the architect of this bleak world. This section provides tools to weave grimdark elements into your storytelling, ensuring your players experience the oppressive reality of Dheghom while not becoming overwhelmed by the brutality of this type of narrative.

    Hope is a Tool

    Even in the darkest of settings, hope has its place. Nothing kills a game faster than a sense of never-ending hopelessness. Hope gives meaning to suffering and a reason to endure. In Dheghom, moments of hope, no matter how fleeting, provide contrast to the seeming malevolence of the world. Rare acts of kindness, signs of redemption, uncertain mercy; these moments make triumphs more rewarding.

    Decisions Have Consequences

    A grimdark world thrives on the ripples caused by every choice. Saving a village from a tyrant might leave it exposed to raiders when the powers that defended it are gone. Burning a factory to save a grove could starve hundreds in the winter. Let these outcomes unfold naturally, ensuring players see the repercussions of heedless heroics.

    Morality Is a Luxury

    NPCs in Dheghom operate in shades of gray, where the right decision may still have horrific consequences and the cruel decision may result in more forgiving outcomes. Create dilemmas where ideals clash with practicality, and survival comes at the cost of innocence. Idealism is often tragic. For instance, will the party attempt to free the healthy from a plague-ridden district and risk spreading the illness or allow them to suffer in quarantine?

    The Players Shape the Story

    Grimdark stories are character-driven. Allow the wounds of your PCs (physical, emotional, or moral) to become part of the narrative. Introduce allies and foes whose flaws mirror the PCs' own, forcing them to confront the kind of people they're becoming. Every choice should leave a mark, not just on the world, but on the PCs themselves, making their journey as much an internal struggle as an external one.

    Collaborate on Darkness

    Grimdark explores themes of violence, corruption, and despair, but it's vital to ensure all players are comfortable engaging with such material. Before beginning your campaign, discuss boundaries and expectations to ensure everyone is willing to explore the setting's darker elements. A shared understanding will allow the story to delve deeper into grimdark without alienating anyone.

    Content Warning

    Dheghom is a world where war, greed, and moral collapse infects everything. It is a setting designed to explore the depravity of unchecked power, the exploitation of the weak, and the fragility of survival. The tales within these lands are meant to challenge PCs, forcing them to navigate a world that offers few victories and far-reaching consequences.

    The grimdark themes in Dheghom focus on the pitilessness of existence, not as a reflection of real-world suffering but as a narrative tool to build compelling stories. Whether it's choosing between the starvation of a village and the desecration of sacred land, or surviving the tyranny of industrial overlords, these themes are designed for character immersion, not player discomfort.

    This book explores grimdark themes that reflect the ugliest aspects of Dheghom. These include systemic oppression, moral corruption, and the futility of existence. Players may encounter scenarios related to trauma, starvation, forced labor, crimes against children, mutilation, desecration, cannibalism, suffering, body horror, and war crimes. Take the time to speak with your group about what they are comfortable with.

    A Fragile Creation

    Dheghom was meant to be a sanctuary, a world crafted with precision and sealed from the chaos beyond. In its earliest days, it was a place of abundance, where gods and mortals existed in harmony, and magic sustained every corner of creation. But perfection is fragile, and even the most carefully designed systems carry the seeds of their own failure.

    This world, cut off from everything beyond itself, was sustained by the gods who created it. Yet, the very design that gave Dheghom its strength, also made it vulnerable. As the centuries passed, cracks began to form; first imperceptible, then catastrophic. Magic faltered, gods crumbled, and mortals were left to wander through the wreckage of a flawed and failing system. What remains now is a world that was never truly whole, spiraling into entropy, haunted by the echoes of its creators' mistakes.

    The Creation and Collapse of Dheghom

    When the gods created Dheghom, they sought to build a perfect world, sealing themselves within it to sustain its magic and life. They tied the world's energy to their own immortal existence, becoming the wellsprings that would nourish all creation. For a time, the system thrived. Magic was abundant, the land was fertile, and mortals flourished under the gods' watchful eyes.

    But the gods misunderstood the beings they chose to seal within. Mortals, given abundance, grew restless and ambitious. The stability of the Bloom Age bred excess and conflict. Mortals turned on each other, and eventually on the gods themselves. Seeing their creators as obstacles or oppressors, mortals attacked the gods, corrupted them, turning them into Tatarigami, monstrous remnants of divinity.

    The corruptions of the gods shattered the balance they had built. Magic, once the sustaining force of Dheghom, began to destabilize. It became chaotic, dangerous, and destructive, and the more gods fell, the more the system unraveled, creating a cascade of devastation. What was once a self-sustaining world became a prison of entropy, spiraling toward collapse.

    Now, mortals live in the wreckage of their creators' design. Magic remains, but it is no longer a blessing, it is a curse that mirrors the decay of the gods. The system built to sustain the world is breaking apart, and there is no force left to repair it. Dheghom was never perfect. It was a flawed creation, doomed by its own design and destroyed by the very beings it sought to protect.

    The Glass That Mortals Cannot See

    Almost all mortals have no concept of the boundaries of their world. To them, Dheghom is all there is, a vast realm crafted by the gods. They do not know that it is sealed, because they do not know there is anything beyond their own world. The idea of an outside, a beyond, is foreign, unspoken, and unknowable. For most, it is beyond comprehension to even suggest that such a thing exists, and for others, it is ridiculous.

    But the system is breaking. As the gods decay and magic falters, the boundaries of Dheghom have begun to fracture. These breaches are rare and unpredictable, but when they occur, they pull in things that have no place in Dheghom. Beings from other planes, bizarre, incomprehensible, and hostile, tear through the fabric of the world, sowing confusion and destruction wherever they appear. To mortals, these intrusions are inexplicable. They have no understanding of planar boundaries or the forces that govern the multiverse. A creature from another plane might be seen as a god, a demon, or something beyond classification entirely. The vast and endless void in which other planes of existence persist is beyond a pane of glass through which mortals cannot see.

    The Fractured Divine

    The gods, over time, bound themselves to physical locations throughout Dheghom, finding solace in landmarks that reflected their nature. Throughout the millennia, they have been weakened as their sanctuaries succumbed to neglect, destruction, or corruption. While their physical forms can be destroyed, what happens to their divine essence remains uncertain to mortals. Some believe it lingers, eventually reforming into a new shape; others fear it could dissipate entirely, robbing the world of their unique divinity. This decay has begun to unravel the natural order, warping landscapes, tainting waters, and birthing monstrous horrors.

    The importance of divine corruption in your campaign depends on how central you want it to be. Its influence can be as immediate or distant as you choose. However, players must be able to connect their characters to the divine or their corruption in order to feel connected to those central themes.

    Focus on the Divine

    If you are interested in developing a campaign or arc on the divine, it is important to help your players find a connection to that idea. Characters with connections to the divine need not be clerics or paladins, but may be of any background or class, since the danger and destruction caused by corrupted gods (Tatarigami), is far reaching and largely inescapable. Character backgrounds, motivations, and ambitions can help to fuel far-reaching adventures throughout your campaign.

    Shadow of the Divine

    If the gods and their woes are not the focus of your player's adventures in Dheghom, you may still hint at the effects their corrupted presence has in the world. Refugees fleeing blighted regions, beasts driven mad with divine fury, or radicals hunting gods. In the forefront of your campaign, there are numerous avenues of adventure that characters can wander along, which do not deal directly with gods or their corruption.

    War. Given that war is nearly a constant state in Dheghom, you can choose to focus adventures on the war efforts in various nations. These adventures can be direct (scouting, battles, peace efforts) or indirect (refugees, deserters, smuggling). A campaign or arc centered on war will oftentimes explore obligation, regret, compromise, survival, and loss.

    Politics. Political campaigns can be as brutal as any war campaign, and in Dheghom, this reality is compounded by the numerous factions vying for control. Campaigns focused around politics can be as straightforward as succession to as sweeping as any of the other avenues for adventure listed here as threats of gods, war, magic, and crime act as your focus. Consider disparities in wealth, power, and technology and themes of corruption, tyranny, and parasitism.

    Crime. In a world where morality begins with unsure footing, it is difficult to pin down what a criminal act really is. That ambiguity is the basis of campaigns and adventures focused on crime, allowing you to pose questions of desperation, pragmatism, and oppression to your players. Numerous factions engage in ostensibly criminal behavior, but oftentimes their status as a criminal organization is determined by those in power rather than their activities being any less wholesome.

    Magic. Most adventuring parties contain at least one wielder of magic, and the strange or dangerous effects of magic will likely draw their attention sooner rather than later. The distrust, fear, and unpredictable phenomena related to the use of magic can create an endless source of fires that need putting out. A campaign centered on magic will inevitably explore the fear, chaos, and devastation caused by its erratic power, affecting both the fates of the adventurer and the world around them.

    The Dheghom Calendar

    The year in Dheghom is measured as 413 days, divided into ten months, each consisting of 40 days. An additional 13-day period, referred to in many cultures as Kraethen, occurs at the end of the year and is tracked separately.

    This calendar was originally developed by the Aos Sí and has been adopted across much of Dheghom. Time is divided further into four tendays per month, with ten days in each tenday.

    The names of the days of the week are: Kaedun, Darun, Triun, Cathrun, Cuigun, Seun, Sechtun, Octun, Naiun, and Deichun. Each day spans a period of 24 hours, though cultural observances of these days vary across regions.

    Holidays

    The holidays of Dheghom are tied to the cycles of the seasons, celestial events, and shared cultural practices across the land. While each nation and culture has its own traditions and festivals, a few universal holidays are recognized in some form across all of Dheghom. These universal celebrations most often mark transitions in the year.

    Ebonwake. The last night before Kraethen is observed with silent lantern processions, the reading of ancestors' names, and food offerings left at thresholds for those who have died.

    First Bloom. The Spring Equinox is celebrated with garland weaving, shared meals of early greens and smoked fish, and the lighting of braziers to "wake the land."

    First Light. The first day of the year is marked with the rekindling of village hearths, the lighting of torches, and the ringing of iron bells to drive away lingering shadows.

    High Sun. During the Summer Solstice, villages gather to light towering pyres to prolong the day, hold contests of strength and skill, and feast until the fires burn out.

    Last Harvest. The Autumn Equinox is celebrated with torchlit races around the fields, offerings of grain and smoked meats, and feasting beneath the fading sun.

    Long Night. The winter solstice is celebrated with raucous gatherings, music, dancing, and drinking, ending in silence as hearths are relit with saved embers to welcome the new year.

    Kraethen. The final 13 days of the year are spent feasting, dancing, hosting competitions, and reveling in defiant celebration before winter's full grip takes hold.

    Midsummer's Rest. The day begins with drinking, and for those willing and drunk enough, games of strength, skill, and fisticuffs are held, with the victor crowned in celebration.

    Moons of Dheghom

    The four moons of Dheghom, each with a distinct orbit and appearance, have shaped how different cultures track time and view celestial phenomena. Some agrarian societies rely on the predictable cycles of Amasal for calendars and agricultural planning, while maritime cultures track the interactions of multiple moons, such as Tharion, Velthora, and Amasal, to predict tides and navigate coastal waters. Eron, faint and overlooked by most, holds significance in certain traditions for its subtle role in alignments. Alignments between moons are almost universally recognized as moments of heightened importance, with some regarded as ill omens due to the floods, storms, and seismic disruptions they often bring.

    Amasal. Amasal is a smooth, pale blue moon, the largest and brightest in the night sky. It completes its cycle every 29 days and its phases are easy to track. Amasal's steady glow dominates the sky when full, casting enough light to illuminate the land.

    Eron. Eron is a small, faint moon, often difficult to see without clear skies. Its dull, grayish surface looks smooth from a distance but appears dim and muted compared to the other moons. Completing its cycle every 36 days, Eron is most noticeable during alignments, when its position in the sky brings it closer to the brighter moons.

    Tharion. Tharion is a reddish moon with a jagged, uneven shape. Its surface has darker streaks and shadowed ridges, making it appear rough and irregular compared to the smoother moons. Completing its orbit in 43 days, Tharion's phases are distinct due to its uneven edges, which create irregular crescents and shadows as it waxes and wanes. Its reddish hue is faint but visible even when partially illuminated.

    Velthora. Velthora is a distant moon with a faint golden glow, appearing smaller and softer than Amasal or Tharion. Its surface looks smoother and more uniform, without obvious shadows or markings, and it takes 57 days to complete its orbit. Velthora's long cycle and subtle light make it striking when full but difficult to see when waning or crescent.

    Climate and Weather

    The continents of Dheghom, Anterra and Oskara, experience the changing of the seasons in vastly different ways, shaped by their distinct geography and environments.

    Anterra

    The eastern continent experiences a wide variety of climates, ranging from the arid deserts of Gialar to the boreal forests and tundras of Sharasa. The southern regions, like Ryhoreiral and Khunland, enjoy mild winters and long growing seasons, though heavy rains in spring can turn the grasslands into floodplains. Avenar and Marcia, located along the coast of the Long Deep, endure blustery winds in spring and autumn, while summer storms frequently batter their shores.

    Oskara

    The western continent faces harsher extremes. The deserts of Baleros and Korath are scorched during the summer and raked by cold, biting winds in winter. The deciduous forests of Bethune erupt in vibrant colors in autumn, though the central mountains of Steinlund shield much of the interior from harsh winter storms. The boreal forests of Tytherin, Keld, and Ibunor face short summers followed by long, brutal winters, with frost often clinging to the ground year-round in the farthest reaches. Along the coastlines, Tuinleas and Gane experience milder seasons, though storms from the Long Deep bring violent winds in spring and autumn.

    The Long Deep Islands

    The islands of Acadiana, Brynnalyn, Kimara and Shirasa, situated in the Long Deep, experience milder seasons overall but are frequently battered by powerful storms. Brynnalyn faces blustery spring rains and humid summers. Acadiana suffers through long, wet winters with torrential downpours but enjoys extended, temperate summers ideal for trade and fishing. The southern islands serve as vital hubs between the continents but endure the constant threat of tempests and unpredictable ocean weather. In the north, the tundras of Sharasa and Kimara remain blanketed in snow for most of the year, and summer is little more than a fleeting thaw.
