# Faction Reputation

Reputation within a faction determines a character’s standing, influence, and access to resources. It is earned through loyalty, competence, and advancing the faction’s interests. Alternatively, reputation is lost through failure, disobedience, or betrayal. Most characters begin with neutral reputation unless prior ties or deeds dictate otherwise.

Reputation is a currency as much as a measure of standing. A trusted agent may leverage their reputation for favors, protection, or privileged information, but reputation spent is not easily regained. Covert actions can build standing in secret, while public victories or humiliations shape perception.

Reputation is not static. It shifts based on success, discretion, and the faction’s changing priorities. Falling out of favor can mean demotion, exile, or death, while rising too quickly may earn the envy of rivals.

## Reputation Increases

A character’s reputation rises when their actions strengthen the faction. Success can be public, earning immediate rewards but drawing envy and scrutiny, or covert, accumulating favor in secret until it proves valuable.

Faction-specific contributions provide the most direct gains. A Zaharmak rebel who cripples a Poke factory earns admiration among compatriots, but also attention from enemies. A Navtog merchant securing an exclusive trade deal demonstrates their worth, but success breeds rivals.

Not all contributions are obvious. Even seemingly mundane efforts can improve standing if they align with faction priorities. A Celtumpra mage frequently donating reagents strengthens research efforts, while a Navrow smuggler running the same minor route provides consistent support and income. Leaders often take note of those who quietly reinforce the faction’s foundation.

## Reputation Decreases

A character’s reputation suffers when their actions harm their faction’s interests, violate its principles, or result in failure. The severity of the loss depends on visibility and consequence: public disgrace leads to immediate backlash, while covert betrayals remain hidden until exposed, often bringing far worse retribution.

Consider how you want a faction to address failures when they arise, whether for your players or for NPCs. A Zaharmak agent failing to execute a sabotage may just be seen as weak, while a Colderyn operative caught selling industrial secrets could face exile or execution. Some factions punish disloyalty swiftly, while others exploit failures as leverage, forcing compromised members into deeper service.

### Covert Reputation

Sometimes your players will get involved in espionage, whether they are catching a spy or are the spies themselves. Covert actions result in reputation, but the tally of this reputation should remain hidden until the espionage is either complete or exposed so that credit can be given to the individuals involved

While acting covertly, an operative’s standing may remain unchanged until exposed or extracted. A Navrow spy embedded in The Navtog may gain public standing by completing missions while secretly building hidden reputation with Navrow. If their deception is uncovered, their reputation with The Navtog collapses, and retribution follows: imprisonment, exile, or death. Meanwhile, their standing with Navrow might rise, their success proving their worth. However, an exposed spy, even one welcomed home, remains a target. Enemy factions may know their face, their methods, and their weaknesses, making them a risk that must be carefully managed. Be prepared for these eventualities in case of failure. Meanwhile, players should weigh the consequences of such actions carefully, knowing that the longer they remain undiscovered, the greater both risks and rewards could be.

### Spending Reputation

Reputation within a faction is a valuable resource that can be spent to request goods, services, or assistance beyond what is normally available. Spending reputation represents calling in a favor or leveraging one’s standing within the faction, but it comes at a cost. The act of spending reputation reduces the character’s influence and standing unless the favor directly benefits the faction.

For example, a Celtumpra druid might request rare alchemical ingredients from another faction member. If they have the coin to pay outright, their reputation remains unaffected. However, if they lack the funds, they can expend reputation to secure the boon, effectively saying, “Do me this favor.” This represents a direct loss of standing within the faction, as they are seen as drawing on its resources without immediate repayment. Alternatively, the druid might frame the request as, “Do me this favor, and I’ll owe you one,” deferring the cost. In this case, their reputation remains intact, but they take on a debt that must be repaid, either by returning the value of the boon or performing a service of equal importance within a given timeframe. Failure to repay could result in more significant reputation loss later, reflecting the faction’s disappointment or frustration.

Balancing the expenditure of reputation requires careful decision-making. Players must consider whether the benefits of calling in a favor outweigh the potential loss of influence within the faction for their characters. Excessive or poorly justified requests may result in diminished trust and reduced access to future resources.

## Separation of Reputation and Role

Reputation and role within a faction are distinct concepts that do not always align. Reputation is how a character is perceived within the faction, based on their deeds, reliability, and alignment with faction goals. Role, however, is the character’s formal or informal position, defining their responsibilities and authority. A Colderyn overseer might enjoy a high reputation for their exceptional work ethic but remain a low-level industrial manager with minimal influence over broader operations. Conversely, a reclusive Celtumpra Luminary might command their own enclave but suffer from a poor reputation due to unorthodox practices or personal grudges.

This mismatch between reputation and role creates unique dynamics within factions. Characters with high reputation but low authority can leverage their influence to gain the trust of peers and leadership, potentially advancing their standing over time. However, they may face frustration when their reputation exceeds their formal power, limiting their access to resources or decision-making. On the other hand, those with significant roles but poor reputations, such as a Zaharmak Plotter resented by their Beaters, Boomers, and Bangers, must contend with internal resistance, undermining, or outright defiance, despite their positional power.


### Players in Different Factions

When your players belong to different factions, while you may be setting your game up for great roleplaying and intricate narratives, you are also laying the groundwork for incredible dysfunction. Before you begin, ensure your group is prepared for character conflict without player animosity. Regular out-of-game communication is essential to maintaining this boundary.

### Espionage and Intrigue

Spies create compelling narrative tools in faction-based campaigns. Place NPC spies within your players' own faction to generate internal threats that can't be solved through direct confrontation. These agents should have specific goals, such as sabotaging operations, leaking information to rivals, or manipulating leadership decisions to benefit their true masters.

For players who wish to act as spies, provide clear objectives through private communications. These missions should force difficult choices between faction loyalty and party cohesion. The most effective spy missions aren't simple intelligence gathering but operations that force players to decide what to do with sensitive information. When a player uncovers evidence of Colderyn's slave labor practices, they must choose between exposing these atrocities or maintaining their cover to serve their faction's larger goals.

Structure espionage in three distinct phases: infiltration, operation, and extraction. Each phase should present unique challenges with clear consequences for failure. Use physical props like coded messages or recognition phrases to immerse players in the espionage experience. Track what information each faction possesses and how it spreads when security is compromised.

The consequences of spy activities should directly impact faction relationships and resources. When players steal Poke manufacturing secrets, the faction might increase security at all facilities, making future infiltration more difficult. If they assassinate a Navtog informant, rival networks might unite against the perceived threat. These cascading effects create a dynamic world where espionage drives meaningful change rather than existing in isolation.

### Practical Implementation

Provide a binding reason why these characters remain united despite their differences. This might be mutual debt, shared blackmail, or an alliance of necessity against a greater threat. The consequence of separation should be worse than the discomfort of cooperation.

Don't shy away from messy situations. Let characters argue and compromise, as this tension creates memorable roleplay. Expect and plan for factional loyalty to eventually break a mission and use that failure as a narrative turning point rather than a game-ending hurdle.

Create safety valves where tensions can be aired without destroying the group. Neutral ground, respected mediators, or downtime activities can provide these necessary release mechanisms. Meanwhile, ensure every player's faction receives spotlight time by rotating the focus to prevent any single faction from dominating the story.

Remember, these conflicts reflect the moral ambiguity and transactional nature of loyalty itself. Use private objectives, staged revelations, and natural consequences to add variability and interest to your campaign. The most compelling stories emerge when players must weigh their factional duties against the bonds they've formed with their companions.

#### Practical Guidelines

While the specifics of how to implement reputation changes are at your discretion, keep in mind that the methods employed by characters should affect how reputation is gained. For instance, ruthless tactics that strengthen standing in The Poke, for example, might alienate Celtumpra’s more principled leadership.

**Frequency of Reputation Changes.** Update reputation whenever characters take a significant action that directly affects the faction’s interests.

- This includes completing missions, public acts of loyalty or defiance, or being directly involved in faction conflicts.

- Minor actions might not immediately change reputation but can accumulate over time.

**Magnitude of Reputation Changes.** The amount of reputation gained for activities will vary based on how quickly you want your players to gain reputation or have it available to spend.

- Minor Impact: +1 or -1 (small favors, minor offenses, successful interactions).

- Moderate Impact: +3 or -3 (completing standard missions, public endorsement or criticism, minor failures).

- Major Impact: +5 or -5 (significant successes or failures).

- Severe Impact: +10 or -10 (major victories, public heroism or disgrace).

- Extreme Cases: Actions like betrayal, internal revolts, or catastrophic failures may result in complete loss of reputation, being hunted by their own faction, or other severe consequences. These situations are at your discretion and should reflect the gravity of the act.

**Tracking Reputation.** Tracking should be as straightforward as tallying the totals and may be something you ask a player to assist with. It may be beneficial to include notes on why changes occurred so players understand the consequences (both good and bad) of their actions.

- Let players know when their reputation changes and why.

- Describe how NPCs react differently based on the new reputation level.
