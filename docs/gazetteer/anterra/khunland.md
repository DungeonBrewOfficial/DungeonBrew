### **History**

The Fomorian Seanachaí \[SHAN-a-khee] tell of their arrival in Dheghom as refugees from a destroyed world where they were subjugated by the Aos Sí. The two peoples made a pact during their exodus that promised freedom to the Fomorians upon their arrival in the new world. During the Bloom Age, the Fomorians developed a clan-based society led by Ríthe \[REE-heh] and guided by Seanachaí to help preserve the oral history of their former oppression. Their religious sect, the Draíodóir \[dree-uh-DOR], guided the construction of massive megalithic stoneworks, which served as religious sanctums and territorial markers of their new freedom.

The peace ended during the Blood Age due to growing territorial pressures. The Fomorians resisted what they perceived as renewed Aos Sí aggression, defending the borders and megalithic sites they had established. The conflict escalated with the arrival of Avenarian refugees. Viewing the humans as invaders on their lands, the Fomorians met them with hostility, which drove the Avenarians to form a military alliance with the Aos Sí. This event locked the Fomorians into a two-front war against both their ancient oppressors and new human adversaries.

Throughout the Blight Age, the strategic situation deteriorated for the Fomorians. The Aos Sí, weakened by rising Aetheric Saturation that disrupted their magic and lacking a centralized military structure, became dependent on their Avenarian allies. The Avenarian military took the lead in the war, conquering Fomorian territories and seizing control of their stoneworks. In response, the Fomorians adapted, refining their tactics, exploiting the weakness of the Aos Sí, and halted the advance.

The current war is a stalemate. Fomorian clans are engaged in a perpetual, two-front conflict, fighting to reclaim lost lands and occupied megaliths from the Avenarians while simultaneously resisting the Aos Sí. The Seanachaí tell the histories of their ancient subjugation as one endless war of freedom and justice, unifying the clans against what they present as inevitable re-enslavement. The entire Fomorian society is now organized around this conflict, seeing the Avenarians as willing pawns and the Aos Sí as manipulative betrayers.

___

### **Government**

The Fomorians have no permanent central government, instead operating as a decentralized society of independent clans. Their political structure is a fluid, situational hierarchy where authority is defined by specific roles rather than formal, permanent ranks.

The foundational leader is the Rí Túath (King of a Tribe), the chieftain who holds authority over a single clan. While each clan is sovereign, a chieftain whose clan controls a major megalithic site earns the additional, prestigious title of Rí Carrag (King of the Rock), granting them immense spiritual and cultural influence.

For large-scale military campaigns, multiple clans will temporarily unify and acclaim one chieftain as the Cath Rí (Battle King). The Cath Rí's authority is absolute but is strictly limited to the duration of the military campaign. The concept of centralized leader, a Mór Rí (Great King), is a legendary one, mythologized to the point that no Fomorian has attempted to attain it in centuries.

### **Interesting Facts**

- Before any major battle, a clan's Seanachaí performs a ritual recitation of the specific historical betrayals and grievances related to the enemy they are about to face. This practice serves to focus the warband's resolve, transforming the fight from a military engagement into the settling of a specific historical debt.

- Fomorian megaliths are primarily constructed from volcanic basalt and andesite, stones too hard for most cultures to quarry or shape effectively. The Draíodóir are not just priests but also master masons who are skilled at splitting and moving these massive stones. This skill makes their structures a source of cultural pride and a mystery to outsiders.

- While known for their massive stonework, Khunland's artisans are also master gem crafters. The nation's volcanic mountains and jungles are a rich source of valuable materials, including jet, obsidian, peridot, and jade. Fomorian crafters carve and polish these materials into intricate jewelry and ceremonial items. These rare goods are a crucial export, providing the Fomorians with a trade link to the outside world.

### **Current Challenges**

- **The Two-Front War.** The Fomorians are locked in a two-front war. To their south, they face the Aos Sí in a series of low-intensity border skirmishes. The primary conflict, however, is a grinding war of attrition to the west against the more numerous and organized Avenarian armies, who now occupy many of the Fomorians' ancient megalithic sites.

- **The Loss of Culture.** The multi-millennial war has begun to consume the very culture the Fomorians are fighting to protect. The peace and freedom they established during the Bloom Age are now distant memories, replaced by a society where every aspect of life is subordinate to the war effort. As a result, in their long fight to escape subjugation, they have shackled themselves to a new kind of slavery: a cultural obligation to fight and die for a cause that has no foreseeable end.

- **The War of the Ríthe.** The military stalemate has created a crisis of leadership, leading to a new, internal conflict. Believing that only a single, absolute ruler can break the deadlock, several powerful Ríthe have begun to maneuver for dominance, each seeking to become the first Mór Rí in centuries. This struggle for power has fractured the clans, leading to a shadow war of assassinations, betrayals, and broken alliances that threatens to shatter the Fomorian war effort from within.

### **Fomorian Characters**

As you create a Fomorian character or NPC, consider the following:

**The Seanachaí's Truth.** Your identity is shaped by the oral histories of the Seanachaí, who sing of ancient betrayals and a long-lost freedom. Do you accept these epic tales as literal truth, a source of righteous inspiration for your struggle? Or does the day-to-day reality of the war create a cynical disconnect from these grand, mythologized stories? Is your history an inheritance or a burden?

**Clan and King.** A Fomorian's first loyalty is to their clan's Rí. Is your chieftain's word law, even if it harms the greater war effort? Ambitious leaders have begun a secret "War of the Ríthe" to claim the title of Mór Rí. Are you a loyal soldier in this power struggle, or do you see it as a betrayal of your people?

**A Purpose Beyond the Spear.** Fomorian society is organized for war, but not all Fomorians are warriors. What is your role? Are you among Draíodóir, trying to build and preserve while the world is torn apart by conflict? Are you an artisan, whose crafts help to fund the war effort? Do you find honor in this work, or do you resent being overlooked in favor of warriors?

### **Religious Practices**

Fomorian faith, like the gods themselves, is anchored to the land. For a people who saw themselves delivered from slavery and oppression upon coming to this new world, they identify with the gods on a personal level and, just as the gods have their sanctums, so too do the Fomorians. Khunland itself is their sanctum, and they see its loss as a violation of their faith and divine connection to their deities.

Religious authority is held by the Draíodóir, a class of priest-architects who lead all rituals and oversee the construction of the massive megalithic stoneworks built to honor their gods and the land. The Fomorians venerate the deities whose sanctums remain within their territory, such as Dubhán and Sruth-Tarbh, and view the corruption of Cromag's spirit into a Tatarigami as another grievance to be laid at the feet of their enemies. This conflict has transformed their faith into a military imperative: reclaim occupied territory and liberate their inaccessible gods.

### **Cities and Sites of Interest**

The geography of Khunland is renowned for its ancient megalithic stoneworks. These monuments are found throughout its territories, from the mangrove swamps and river deltas of its coasts to the inland savannahs and jungles. The nation is bordered by the Aos Sí to the south and Avenarians to the west and the Tregintir Wastes to the east. Many of the megalithic sites have been transformed into defensive fortifications as a part of their multi-front war.

#### **_Gizar_**

The capital of Khunland is a fortified port city on the coast of the Sea of Khun at the delta of the Abhainn Eala. The city is built with massive stones of basalt and andesite, the city's landward side is protected by thick walls and defensive towers. Its harbor is enclosed by sea walls that shelter its ships. Gizar serves as the primary military and political hub for the clans, a place where warbands gather and supplies for the western front are marshaled.

#### **_The Tregintir Wastes_**

This blighted land forms a natural barrier between Khunland, Gialar, and Marcia. The wasteland was created by the Tatarigami Urquoth after it was consumed by the Kapre. Now a landscape of dust and bone, the region is inhabited by Urquoth and the spectral Preta born from its curse. The only structures that remain are the crumbling megaliths of deities lost to the blight. The Draíodóir often send expeditions to seek out these lost deities, hoping to save them from destruction of Urquoth.

#### **_Loch Eala_**

This large lake on the western border is the primary battlefield in the war against the Aos Sí and Avenarians. Its proximity to Ayos, Gizar, and Inveleral makes it a key strategic location. The loch is also the sanctum of the corrupted deity Cromag, forcing the Fomorians to not only fight their mortal enemies but also to contain the actions of their maddened god.

### **Ríthe**

Fomorian society is led by a fluid hierarchy of Ríthe (Kings) whose titles reflect their specific function, prestige, or temporary authority. These roles are not a formal ladder of advancement but a series of situational responsibilities that define the Fomorian power structure.


#### **_Rí Túath_**

The Rí Túath \[REE TOO-ah] (King of a Tribe) is the foundational leader of a single Fomorian clan, holding direct political and military authority over their people. Advised by a clan's Seanachaí (lorekeeper), the Rí Túath's rule is legitimized by the oral histories that preserve the Fomorian identity and justify their endless war.


#### **_Rí Carrag_**

A Rí Carrag \[REE KA-rig] (King of the Rock) is a Rí Túath whose clan controls a major megalithic site. This is a title of immense prestige, granting the chieftain significant spiritual and cultural influence as a guardian of such a location. While a Rí Carrag is highly respected and often deferred to in matters of tradition, the title does not confer formal command over other clans.


#### **_Cath Rí_**

The Cath Rí \[KAH REE] (Battle King) is a temporary military commander recognized by multiple clans before a large-scale campaign. The chieftain chosen to be Cath Rí is granted authority over the allied war effort. However, this power is strictly limited to the duration of the campaign and dissolves the moment the objective is met or the alliance fractures, at which point the chieftain reverts to their original standing.


#### **_Mór Rí_**

The Mór Rí \[MORE REE] (Great King) is a legendary and historical title, not a current political office. In the past, a ruthless chieftain could seize the title through the bloody conquest and unification of other clans. If this consolidation of power led to great victories, the Seanachaí would retroactively mythologize the king into a larger-than-life figure. This very process, however, has made the title culturally unattainable in the modern era, as the legends are now so immense that any Rí attempting to claim the title would be seen as impossibly arrogant.
