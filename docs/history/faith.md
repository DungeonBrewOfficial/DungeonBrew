# Faith in Dheghom

Religion in Dheghom is as varied and complex as the lands and peoples that inhabit it. The gods are not distant or abstract; they are tangible, physical beings bound to their domains. How communities interact with their deities or faith is expressed through corporeal relationships, whether positive or negative, or the void left behind by beings that have vanished or transformed into something unrecognizable: Tatarigami.

### ***A Tangible Divinity***

The tangible, and therefore fallible, nature of the gods makes belief a volatile and deeply personal affair. The same reverence that fosters unity within a community is always shadowed by the reality that a god can be corrupted or killed. This truth drives the spectrum of mortal response, from outright rejection of the divine as dangerous relics, to a desperate, clinging faith in them as the only bulwark against a chaotic world.

### ***Cultural Worship & The Priesthood***

Worship is always rooted in local culture and geography, tailored to the specific nature of a resident deity, such as the Kapre reverence for the great vulture Gialar, or the Dwergar's continued honor for the now-corrupted Váttkorr. This fragmentation of belief extends to the priesthood, creating deep mistrust between regions. A cleric whose divine connection manifests as magical power would be condemned as a charlatan or a witch by communities where priests are expected to be non-magical leaders.

### ***The Sanctum: A Physical Anchor***

Every deity in Dheghom is inextricably bound to a single, natural geographic location. This sanctum, be it a specific mountain, a forest grove, or a stretch of coastline, is not merely a home but a physical extension of the deity itself. The entity's stability and health are directly linked to the integrity of this chosen place. Physical harm to the sanctum through pollution, logging, or desecration is a direct wound inflicted upon the deity, and a sufficient violation can cause the transformation detailed in the following chapter on Tatarigami.

### ***Echoes of the Divine***

A god’s influence lingers even where active worship has ceased. Remnants of old beliefs persist in rituals, symbols, and traditions, shaping communities long after the deities they honor have faded. Whether a god is present, absent, or corrupted, its mark on the world is felt across Dheghom.

## ***Foundations of Belief***

To ground a character's relationship with faith, use the following tables for inspiration.

#### Foundations of Belief

|  |  |
| ----- | :---- |
| **d6** | **Impact** |
| 1 | You were raised with faith tied to a forgotten god or dead deity, yet their teachings persist through rituals and stories handed down by your family. |
| 2 | You survived an encounter with a Tatarigami, leaving you convinced of the power of gods, both benevolent and corrupted. |
| 3 | You were born during a significant celestial event, and elders marked you as chosen by the gods, even if you’ve never felt their presence. |
| 4 | A wandering mystic or hermit inspired you to seek out the divine, introducing you to a faith you had never encountered before. |
| 5 | You found an artifact or relic tied to a forgotten faith, its power or mystery driving you to uncover the truths of the divine. |
| 6 | A god intervened directly in your life, saving you from death or catastrophe. Whether this intervention was intentional or accidental remains unclear. |

#### Rejection of Faith

|  |  |
| ----- | :---- |
| **d6** | **Impact** |
| 1 | You believe the gods are fallible creatures, no better than mortals, and unworthy of worship or devotion. |
| 2 | Your family once served a god that has since become a Tatarigami, and you see their fall as proof of divine corruption and imperfection. |
| 3 | You were raised without any exposure to faith or gods and view belief as a crutch for those unwilling to face reality. |
| 4 | A god’s physical presence, whether majestic or monstrous, terrified you as a child, leaving you scarred and distrustful of divine power. |
| 5 | You grew up in a community abandoned by their deity, and you see faith as futile, a relic of a forgotten past. |
| 6 | You witnessed a god’s apathy in the face of suffering, convincing you that the divine is indifferent to mortal lives. |
