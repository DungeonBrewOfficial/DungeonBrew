#### A Primer on Dogs

```
Generations of breeding have shaped dogs for countless
roles. Hounds track quarry, guard dogs stand watch, working
dogs are reliable and tireless, terriers root out hidden pests,
and companion breeds offer comfort and camaraderie.
Understanding these general tendencies can help flesh out
your dog's behavior and place in the world.
Beyond specific breed instincts, dogs generated with this
system fall into three general categories, reflecting
differences in size, strength, and speed. These builds
influence their capabilities and suitability for certain tasks.
```
##### Standard Breeds

```
These dogs represent the common medium-to-small breeds
often prized for their agility, keen senses, and versatility.
While lacking the raw power of larger dogs, they are often
quicker and may fit into roles requiring speed or navigating
tighter spaces. Many spaniels, terriers, and smaller hounds
fall into this general build.
(Corresponds to Standard Stat Blocks)
```
##### Heavy Breeds

```
Representing sturdy, medium-to-large breeds, these dogs
strike a balance between strength and agility. They possess
good endurance and are capable workers, suitable for a wide
range of tasks from guarding to hunting larger game.
Shepherds, retrievers, and many working breeds fit this build.
(Corresponds to Heavy Stat Blocks)
```
##### Giant Breeds

```
These dogs encompass the largest and most powerful
breeds, selected for sheer strength, intimidation, or
resilience. While potentially less agile than smaller dogs, their
size and power make them suitable for heavy tasks, pulling
loads, or confronting formidable foes. Mastiffs, wolfhounds,
and mountain dogs exemplify this build.
(Corresponds to Giant Stat Blocks)
These categories provide a foundation, but a dog's true role
is often defined by its training and inherent instincts, shaping
them into specialized Guards, Hunters, Scouts, or War Dogs,
which are detailed in the next section.
```
More than just pets, dogs in the adventuring world are
partners. They are the keen senses warning of ambush, the
fierce loyalty guarding a campsite, the joyful bark greeting
friends in a hostile world. From the hardy mastiff pulling a
sled across frozen wastes to the clever terrier sniffing out
traps in a dungeon, a dog's capabilities are as varied as their
breeds.
Not every dog is a pedigree champion, and not every
adventurer seeks one. Some look for unwavering loyalty.
Others want keen senses, ferocity, or reliability. Some just
need a warm body at their feet after a long day's march. This
guide helps you create a unique canine companion, defining
not just its abilities but its spirit, quirks, and the unspoken
bond it shares with those it travels with.

### Using This System

This system offers a framework for generating distinct dog
companions using rollable tables. While the options provide
depth, the process is designed to be intuitive, guiding you
through creating a believable and engaging canine friend.
**Players:**
When acquiring a dog (through purchase, adoption,
rescue, etc.), your GM might use these tables or allow
you to roll yourself.
If a dog is part of your background or starting gear, the
GM can tailor the results to better fit your character's
history.
Dogs with exceptional traits might be rarer, costlier, or
tied to specific quests or discoveries.
**Game Masters:**
Generate dogs quickly for NPCs, populate kennels,
create memorable strays, or design unique animal
companions found in the wild.
Offer players choices by rolling up several distinct dogs
available at a market, found in a monster's lair, or offered
by a breeder.
Give dogs with particularly notable traits a richer
backstory – perhaps it belonged to a famous ranger,
escaped a wizard's lab, or is the last of a noble bloodline.
Feel free to adjust rolls or results to match the tone and
needs of your specific campaign setting.

#### Dog Archetypes

A dog's categorization gives clues to its potential, but its
training dictates how it truly functions as a companion.
Whether trained for vigilance, the hunt, exploration, or battle,
each role shapes a dog's skills, reactions, and overall utility
to an adventuring party. The following archetypes detail
these specialized roles:

##### Guard Dogs

Bred for vigilance and protection, these dogs excel at
watching over a person, place, or object. They are often
territorial and possess keen senses to detect intruders or
danger. While not always aggressive, they are typically brave
and won't hesitate to alert their masters or confront a threat.
Their loyalty is paramount, making them excellent sentinels
for a campsite or keep.
Guard dogs are often trained to stay awake at night to act
as an Alarm spell for the camp.
(See Guard Dog stat blocks).

##### Hunting Dogs

```
These dogs are masters of the chase, bred for stamina and
exceptional senses, particularly smell. They assist hunters in
tracking and sometimes retrieving quarry across vast
distances and varied terrains. In an adventuring party, they
might track monsters, find lost trails, or locate hidden
creatures through scent. Their focus is often on the hunt,
sometimes to the exclusion of other commands.
Hunting dogs are often trained to roam while traveling
and bring “rations” back to their master.
(See Hunting Dog stat blocks).
```
##### Scout Dogs

```
Agile, alert, and often possessing a keen nose for more than
just creature trails, scout dogs are invaluable for
reconnaissance and hazard detection. They might be adept
at moving stealthily, noticing hidden details like tripwires or
pressure plates, or sniffing out poisons, alchemical traps, or
unstable structures. Their smaller size and inquisitive nature
often suit them to exploring ahead or investigating tight
spaces.
Scout dogs are often trained to move ahead of their
master, searching for traps.
(See Scout Dog stat blocks).
```
##### War Dogs

```
Trained for battle, war dogs are typically strong, resilient, and
courageous. They fight alongside their handlers, trained to
charge enemies, harry flanks, or pull down opponents. Their
breeding and training emphasize aggression and obedience
in the chaos of combat, making them formidable assets on
the battlefield, though they may require experienced
handling.
War dogs are often trained to wear armor and participate
in battles.
(See War Dog stat blocks).
```

#### Dog Appearance

```
A dog's appearance is a combination of base coat colors,
genetic modifiers like dilutions, and various patterns or
markings. While some dogs boast a uniform coat, many
display a striking variety of spots, patches, points, and
blended colors. These combinations often reflect a dog's
lineage, regional origins, specific breeding purpose, or even
rarity.
```
##### Wild Pattern

```
Coats mimicking those found in wild canids, often featuring
banded hairs (Agouti) or distinct regional patterns like saddle
markings or countershading. These often provide natural
camouflage.
Agouti - Individual hairs show bands of alternating light
and dark pigment, creating an overall grizzled or ticked
effect across the coat.
Jackal - Features a blending of darker hairs
concentrated along the back that fade into lighter shades
on the underside, muzzle, and legs, without distinct
patches or spots.
Backed - Displays a solid patch of darker color forming a
distinct "saddle" or "cape" marking over the back, clearly
contrasting with lighter colors on the rest of the body.
Side-Striped - Marked by vertical stripes of contrasting
color running along the dog's flanks or sides.
```

##### Markings

Distinct areas of color, often white or tan points, appearing
over the base coat in specific locations like the face, chest, or
legs, or as larger patches.
**Countershaded** - The top surface of the dog (back, top
of head/tail) is darker, while the underside (belly, chest,
throat, under tail) is significantly lighter.
**Urajiro** - A specific form of countershading featuring pale
reddish or whitish markings on the muzzle sides, cheeks,
throat, chest, belly, and inside of legs.
**Nonself** - Refers to white markings appearing on areas
not typically associated with the base color genetics,
often on the chest, toes, or tail tip.
**Dark Mask** - Dark pigmentation (usually black or liver)
covering the muzzle area, sometimes extending up
around the eyes.
**Saddle** - A large patch of darker color (typically black,
liver, or blue) covering the dog's back like a saddle,
distinct from the lighter base color on the rest of the body.
**Minimal White** - Small, often irregular, patches of white
typically appearing on the chest, toes, or tail tip.
**Extreme White** - Large areas of white covering most of
the body, leaving only small patches of the base color,
often on the head or near the tail.
**Mealy** - Features pale, mealy-colored (off-white, pale tan,
or greyish) markings around the muzzle, eyes, belly, and
lower legs, often seen with brown or red base coats.
**Piebald** - Characterized by irregular, often large, patches
of white on a colored background. The colored patches
are clearly defined against the white.
**Extreme Piebald** - Similar to piebald, but the white
covers the vast majority of the body, leaving only very
small, isolated patches of color, sometimes just on the
ears or head.
**Spotted** - Distinct, typically rounded spots of color
distributed over a white base coat. Size and distribution
can vary greatly.
**Watermark** - Subtle, wavy patterns or "watermarks"
appearing within the coat, often slightly lighter or darker
than the surrounding fur, sometimes visible only in
certain light.

##### Brindle

```
Characterized by subtle or bold "tiger stripes" of a darker
color overlaying a lighter base coat, or sometimes reversed.
Black Brindle (Reverse) - A dark base coat (often black
or near-black) overlaid with lighter stripes (like fawn, red,
or silver).
Red Brindle - Features distinct black stripes over a
reddish base coat.
Light Brindle - Paler base coat (like cream or light fawn)
with darker stripes, creating a lower-contrast brindle
pattern.
Brindle - The standard pattern of distinct black stripes
over a fawn or brownish base coat.
Fawn Brindle - Distinct black stripes over a fawn (light
yellowish-tan) base coat.
Dark Brindle - A darker base coat (like deep red or
mahogany) with black stripes, often appearing very dark
overall.
Silver Brindle - Distinct black stripes over a silvery-grey
base coat.
Liver Brindle - Features distinct liver (brown) stripes
over a lighter base coat (often fawn or cream).
Brindle and White - Any brindle pattern combined with
distinct patches of white, typically on the chest, feet, or
face.
Gold Brindle - Distinct black stripes over a rich golden or
yellow base coat.
```

##### Tick and Roan

```
Involves small spots of color (ticking) on white areas, or a
fine mixture of white and colored hairs (roaning) giving a
mottled appearance across parts of the body.
Bluetick - Black ticking (small spots) distributed
throughout a white coat, often giving an overall blueish
impression.
Diluted Bluetick - Similar to bluetick, but the ticking
consists of diluted black (grey or blue) spots on white.
Redtick - Red or orange ticking distributed throughout a
white coat.
Diluted Redtick - Similar to redtick, but the ticking
consists of diluted red (fawn or cream) spots on white.
Black and Tan Tick - A standard black and tan pattern
where the white areas are marked with ticking.
Blue Roan - A fine, close mixture of black and white
hairs, typically giving the coat a blueish-grey or steel
appearance.
Red Roan - A fine, close mixture of red and white hairs,
giving the coat a pinkish or reddish-grey appearance.
Liver Roan - A fine, close mixture of liver (brown) and
white hairs, giving the coat a brownish-grey appearance.
```

##### Body Markings (Bi/Tricolor)

Coats defined by distinct patches of two (bicolor) or three
(tricolor) colors, often in specific, recognizable patterns.
**Sable Tricolor** - A sable main coat (lighter hairs tipped
with black) combined with distinct white markings and tan
points.
**Red/Sable Tricolor** - A red or sable main coat combined
with distinct white markings and tan points.
**Spotted Tricolor** - A primarily white coat marked with
separate, distinct spots of two other colors (commonly
black and tan).
**Liver Tricolor** - A liver (brown) main coat combined with
distinct white markings and tan points.
**Black Tricolor** - A black main coat combined with
distinct white markings and tan points.
**Black and Tan** - Primarily black coat with sharply defined
tan markings in specific locations (e.g., eyebrows,
muzzle, chest, legs).
**Liver and White** - A two-color pattern featuring distinct
patches of liver (brown) on a white background.
**Liver and Tan** - Primarily liver (brown) coat with sharply
defined tan markings in specific locations.

##### Merle

Creates mottled patches where the base color is randomly
diluted, often resulting in unique blue, red, or lilac variations
and sometimes affecting eye color.
**Blue Merle** - Black patches on a grey or blue-grey diluted
background.
**Red/Sable Merle** - Red or sable patches on a lighter,
diluted reddish or tan background.
**Lilac Merle** - Liver (brown) patches on a pale, diluted
lilac or Isabella background.
**Light Merle** - A generally paler version of merle, with
less contrast between the patches and the diluted
background.
**Tan and Red Merle** - A red merle pattern that also
incorporates distinct tan points.
**Tweed Merle** - A variation of merle featuring multiple
shades within the patches and/or background, creating a
more complex, tweed-like effect rather than distinct
mottling.
**Harlequin** - Primarily white coat with irregular, torn-
edged patches of another color. Distinct from piebald or
merle patching.
**Sable Merle** - Sable patches (lighter hairs tipped black)
on a diluted background.
**Liver Merle** - Liver (brown) patches on a lighter, diluted
brownish or tan background.
**Blue Merle and Tan** - A standard blue merle pattern that
also incorporates distinct tan points.
**Agouti Merle** - An agouti base coat (banded hairs) with
the merle pattern overlaid, creating a complex mix of
mottling and banding.
**Double Merle** - Often results in large amounts of white,
with smaller, less distinct merle patches.

##### Training

```
Some Bad or Neutral traits can be removed through
consistent training. Tricks can also be taught in the same
way. At the end of each week of downtime spent training a
dog, a character may attempt one DC 15 Animal Handling
check. After 4 total successes, the trait is removed or the
trick is learned. After 4 total failures, the trait becomes
permanent or the dog can no longer learn that trick. Only one
trait or trick may be trained at a time. Trainable options are
noted in their respective tables.
```
#### Traits

Dog Traits are physical and behavioral qualities that reflect
how a dog performs, behaves, and handles under stress.
Some are beneficial, others are liabilities, and many are just
quirks that come with the breed, training, or the life the dog
has lived. These traits are determined randomly and vary
based on the dog’s age. A dog might be easy to train, prone
to barking, remarkably fast, or always underfoot. Traits have
mechanical effects, but they also serve as narrative cues that
define how a dog feels in play.
