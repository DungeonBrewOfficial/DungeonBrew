# Hoof & Rein

<div class="page-split" markdown="1">

<div class="main-content" markdown="1">

A rider is only as good as the horse beneath them. This system creates more than a stat block — it creates a companion. Your mount has a history, strengths, weaknesses, and quirks. It might be spirited and fast, or it might bite at strangers. Maybe it's unbreakable in battle, or maybe it just refuses to move faster than a canter. Every roll shapes its personality, making each mount part of the journey.

## Using This System

This system provides a structured approach to generating unique horses through a series of rollable tables. Each table feeds into the next, ensuring logical and engaging results without unnecessary complexity.

**Players:** If you are purchasing or acquiring a horse, your GM may either roll randomly or allow you to roll using this system. If a horse is part of your starting equipment, the GM may adjust the results to fit your background. Horses with rare traits or exceptional abilities may come at a premium price, or with attached narrative consequences.

**Game Masters:** Use this system to generate horses in advance or on the fly. For stables, auctions, or breeders, roll multiple horses and let players choose between them. If a mount has particularly rare or valuable traits, consider tying it to a quest, noble house, or infamous rider. Adjust or reroll as needed to fit your setting and campaign tone.

## A Primer on Horses

### Stallions, Mares, and Geldings

<div class="hanging-indent" markdown="1">

**Stallions** are uncastrated male horses. Powerful and aggressive, stallions require experienced handling. They are the natural choice for warhorses because their strength and drive make them suited for combat. However, they can be unpredictable, especially around other horses.

**Mares** are female horses. They tend to be more even-tempered than stallions, but they can be just as stubborn. They are reliable mounts for travel, work, and riding, making up the bulk of horses used by merchants, messengers, and everyday riders. However, mares are rarely chosen for war.

**Geldings** are castrated male horses. They are calmer, steadier, and easier to train than stallions, making them ideal for everyday use. Most common riding horses are geldings because they are predictable and safe for less experienced riders. However, geldings are never warhorses. The same process that makes them manageable also removes the aggression and drive needed for combat.

</div>

This is why warhorses are always stallions in this system. While mares and geldings may be trained for riding, labor, or even racing, war demands something more — a warhorse must have fire and ferocity, traits that only stallions maintain.

</div>

<div class="hanging-indent" markdown="1">

**Donkey or Mule.** Donkeys are small, sure-footed, and durable, often used for short-range hauling or travel through tight or unstable terrain. They are slow but steady, with a calm temperament. Mules, bred from a male donkey and female horse, are larger and stronger. They combine the endurance of a donkey with the size and gait of a horse. *Recommended to use official published stat block.*

**Draft Horse.** Draft horses are large, muscular, and bred for heavy labor. While not as fast as other horses, they are unmatched in sheer strength and endurance. They can carry heavy loads, pull wagons, and even serve as mounts for larger riders. However, they lack the agility of lighter breeds and are not suited for fast-paced combat. *See included stat block.*

**Pony.** Ponies are smaller than full-sized horses but are often just as strong relative to their size. While ponies can be excellent mounts for smaller riders, they sometimes have stubborn personalities that make them challenging to handle. *Recommended to use official published stat block.*

**Riding Horse.** Riding horses are the most common type of horse used for travel. They are bred for speed, endurance, and reliability, making them a preferred choice for merchants, travelers, and messengers. While they lack the strength of draft horses or the aggression of warhorses, they are well-rounded, dependable mounts. *Recommended to use official published stat block.*

**War Horse, Light.** A light war horse is trained for combat but remains faster and more maneuverable than heavier warhorses. These mounts are well-suited for skirmishers, scouts, and archers who need to remain mobile in battle. *See included stat block.*

**War Horse, Medium.** A medium war horse strikes a balance between speed and strength. These horses are trained for battle, capable of charging through enemy lines while still maintaining some mobility. Knights and cavalry often rely on these well-bred warhorses. *See included stat block.*

**War Horse, Heavy.** The largest and most powerful warhorse, bred for battle and capable of carrying a heavily armored rider into the thick of combat. These horses are fearless, aggressive, and bred to trample enemies underfoot. While they are slower than lighter breeds, they are unmatched in raw power. *See included stat block.*

**Wild Horse.** A wild horse is untrained, unpredictable, and wary of people. These horses must be broken before they can be ridden, and even then, they may resist commands or bolt under stress. While some wild horses have impressive stamina and spirit, their untrained nature makes them a risky choice for anyone who needs a reliable mount. *See included stat block.*

<div class="hanging-indent" markdown="1">

**Jet Black** — A deep, glossy black coat without any fading or highlights. Rare and striking.

**Fading Black** — Appears black in winter but fades to brown or rusty tones in the sun. Often mistaken for brown horses.

**Seal Brown** — Nearly black with subtle reddish or mahogany highlights, especially around the flanks and muzzle.

**Brown** — A solid brown coat, typically uniform with no black points. Common among working horses.

**Bay** — Bay horses have a reddish-brown coat with black points (mane, tail, legs). Variants range from pale copper to deep mahogany.

**Chestnut** — Chestnut horses have reddish coats with no black points. Color ranges from pale sorrel to deep liver. Mane and tail usually match or are lighter.

**Gray** — Gray horses lighten with age. Coats may begin dark and slowly become white or silver. Includes dapple, iron, and fleabitten variants.

**Dun Dilute** — Duns have a diluted coat color with a distinct dorsal stripe, leg barring, and primitive markings. Usually dusty or muted in tone.

**Cream Dilute** — Cream dilution creates palominos, buckskins, and other light golden coats. Often results in blue or hazel eyes.

**Champagne Dilute** — Creates soft gold, amber, or pinkish coats with freckled skin and light eyes. Foals are born bright and darken with age.

**Silver Dilute** — Primarily affects black pigment, lightening manes and tails to silver or white. Rare and mostly seen in darker base coats.

**Dilute Combination** — A mix of multiple dilution genes. Produces rare and complex coat colors with layered effects like cream over dun or silver over grulla.

**Horse Patterns** — Overlays of color or markings that appear on top of base coats. Basic Patterns are natural variations that subtly alter a horse's coat. Pinto Patterns are large, irregular patches of white over a base coat. Appaloosa Patterns involve mottling, spots, and blankets of color, typically centered on the hindquarters. Other Markings include rare or unusual features such as freckling, marbling, or lace-like patterns.

## Traits

Horse Traits are physical and behavioral qualities that reflect how a horse performs, behaves, and handles under stress. These traits are determined randomly and vary based on the horse's age. Traits have mechanical effects, but they also serve as narrative cues that define how a horse feels in play.

</div>

<div class="main-content" markdown="1">

### Training

Some Bad or Neutral traits can be removed through consistent training. Tricks can also be taught in the same way. At the end of each week of downtime spent training a horse, a character may attempt one DC 15 Animal Handling check. After 4 total successes, the trait is removed or the trick is learned. After 4 total failures, the trait becomes permanent or the horse can no longer learn that trick. Only one trait or trick may be trained at a time. Trainable options are noted in their respective tables.

</div>

</div>
